Which concept fits the brand narrative?

The narrative has evolved. The original framing was "we build production infrastructure" — a maker identity. The updated direction is broader: O&G as enablers of energy production. Think Palantir — they don't say "we build data software," they say "we enable organisations to integrate data, decisions, and operations." The logo needs to signal capability and enablement, not just fabrication.

Concept A (Refined Engineering) — the interlocking gears carry a strong "integration" story. Two systems meshing = we bring systems together. This still works under an enabler framing, but it skews toward the "builder" end of the spectrum. Strongest for technical audiences and bid documents.

Concept B (Hexagonal Precision) — the hexagonal form is abstract and premium, but it's generic. It doesn't say "energy" or "engineering" or "enablement." It could be any industry. Weakest narrative fit.

Concept C (Typographic Integration) — the gear-as-O is clean, modern, and abstract enough to grow with a broadening identity. It says "engineering" without saying "fabrication shop." As O&G expands into water treatment, gas compression, containerised power, and sustainability-linked products, this mark won't feel narrow. The short mark (⚙&G) is the most distinctive of the three.

Recommendation

For the enabler positioning: Concept C. It's broad enough for where the company is going, specific enough to still read as engineering, and the most elegant system (the icon and wordmark are inseparable). Concept A is the right choice if the team wants to emphasise integration and end-to-end delivery over the broader enabler identity.
